Ask most people how they're doing financially and they'll tell you their salary. But income is a rate, not a result. You can earn a fortune and own nothing. The number that actually measures financial progress is **net worth** — and tracking it is the closest thing personal finance has to a cheat code.

## What net worth actually is

Net worth = **everything you own − everything you owe.** Total your assets (cash, investments, retirement accounts, property, vehicles at realistic value), subtract your liabilities (mortgage, car loans, credit cards, student loans), and the result is your financial bottom line. It's the number that would remain if you sold everything and paid off every debt.

Our [net worth calculator](/calculators/net-worth-calculator/) does the arithmetic; the discipline of tracking it is where the value lies.

## Why it beats income as a scorecard

Income measures how fast you're rowing. Net worth measures where the boat has actually gotten to. The two often disagree:

- A $200,000 earner who spends $210,000 has a *shrinking* net worth despite a big salary.
- A $60,000 earner who lives on $45,000 and invests the rest has a *growing* net worth despite modest income.

Over time, net worth exposes what income hides: whether you're actually building wealth or just moving large amounts of money through your hands. This is why two people with identical salaries can end up decades apart in financial security — the difference shows up in net worth long before it shows up anywhere else.

## Liquid vs total net worth

One nuance matters: not all net worth is spendable. Your home is usually your largest asset but the hardest to access — you can't sell a bedroom to cover an emergency. That's why it's worth tracking **liquid net worth** (cash and investments minus debts) separately. A large total net worth that's almost entirely home equity can coexist with a genuine cash crunch. Liquid net worth is the truer measure of financial flexibility.

## The tracking habit

The power isn't in calculating net worth once — it's in tracking the trend:

1. **Pick a date** (say, the first of each quarter) and calculate net worth the same way every time.
2. **Record it** in a simple spreadsheet or app. Consistency of method matters more than precision.
3. **Watch the slope, not the number.** Is the line rising? Accelerating? Flat despite good income? The trajectory is the diagnosis.

Quarterly is ideal for most people — frequent enough to catch problems, infrequent enough to avoid obsessing over market noise. Monthly can feel jumpy because of investment swings; annually can let a bad year run too long before you notice.

## What the trend tells you

- **Steadily rising:** your financial system works. Keep doing what you're doing.
- **Flat despite a good income:** lifestyle inflation or hidden debt is absorbing everything you earn. This is the most common and most fixable problem — and net worth is often the only place it shows up clearly.
- **Falling:** debt is outpacing assets. Time to attack liabilities and cut spending before it compounds.
- **Volatile but climbing:** normal for anyone with significant investments. Zoom out; the multi-year slope is what counts.

## What to count, honestly

Where people cook their own books: **cars** belong at what a dealer would actually pay (trade-in value, not what you spent — a $45,000 car is often a $30,000 asset the next morning); **the home** at a conservative market estimate, and remember selling costs 6-8% (agent commissions and closing costs), so the equity is smaller than the Zillow-minus-mortgage math suggests; **retirement accounts** at face value for trend purposes, though pre-tax balances carry an embedded tax bill (a $500,000 traditional 401(k) is maybe $400,000 of spendable money — the [RMD](/calculators/rmd-calculator/) and withdrawal taxes are real); **collectibles, points, furniture** — round to zero unless you'd genuinely sell them. Pensions and Social Security are wealth-like income streams; most people track them separately rather than capitalizing them. The rule underneath: net worth is only a useful instrument if it would survive an auditor with a straight face. Flattering numbers defeat the entire diagnostic purpose.

## Milestones worth noticing

Net worth has satisfying inflection points: the day it crosses zero (assets finally exceed debts), the first $100,000 (famously the hardest, because compounding has little to work with early on), and the point where investment growth in a good year exceeds what you contributed — the moment your money starts out-earning your effort. None of these are visible in a paycheck. All of them are visible in a tracked net worth.

A useful external benchmark, held loosely: the median U.S. household net worth is roughly $190,000, but it varies enormously by age — median households cross $100,000 in their late 30s, $200,000 in their late 40s, and peak near $400,000 around retirement. Comparisons motivate some people and corrode others; if the numbers race is the corroding kind, compare only against your own line from last year. The slope you control beats the percentile you don't.

## Bottom line

Income is the engine; net worth is the odometer. Calculate it honestly, track it on a fixed schedule, and let the trend — not any single number — guide your decisions. It's the one metric that tells the truth about whether you're actually getting ahead, and the simple act of watching it tends to make the line go up.
