Buying your first home is exciting, expensive, and full of unfamiliar terms and hidden costs. This guide walks through the whole journey — from saving the deposit to getting the keys — so you go in informed rather than overwhelmed.

## Step 1: Figure out what you can actually afford

Before falling in love with listings, know your budget. Lenders use the **28/36 rule**: keep housing costs under 28% of gross monthly income, and total debt under 36% (see [how much house you can afford](/articles/how-much-house-can-you-afford/)). Our [home affordability calculator](/calculators/home-affordability-calculator/) turns your income, debts, and down payment into a sensible maximum price.

Crucial mindset: the amount a lender *approves* is a ceiling, not a target. Buying below your max leaves room for savings, emergencies, and life — the difference between comfortable homeownership and being "house-poor."

## Step 2: Save the down payment (and more)

You'll need cash for several things, not just the down payment:

- **Down payment** — ideally 20% to avoid private mortgage insurance (PMI) and get better rates, though many first-timers put down less (see [how to save for a down payment](/articles/how-to-save-for-a-house-down-payment/) and the [down payment calculator](/calculators/house-down-payment-calculator/)).
- **Closing costs** — typically 2–5% of the price for loan fees, appraisal, title, and taxes. Easy to forget and painful to be short on.
- **Emergency fund** — keep it *intact*. Don't drain your safety net to buy; a home springs expensive surprises the moment you own it.
- **Moving and immediate costs** — furniture, repairs, deposits.

Keep this money in safe, liquid savings, never the stock market, since you'll need it on a fixed timeline.

## Step 3: Get your finances loan-ready

Lenders scrutinize two things especially:

- **Credit score** — a higher score means a lower rate, which saves tens of thousands over the loan. Check and improve it well before applying (see [how to build credit](/articles/how-to-build-credit/) and keep [utilization](/calculators/credit-utilization-calculator/) low).
- **Debt-to-income ratio** — pay down debts and avoid new ones (no new car loans or cards right before applying), since [DTI](/articles/debt-to-income-ratio-explained/) heavily influences approval.

## Step 4: Get pre-approved

A mortgage **pre-approval** is a lender's written estimate of how much they'll lend you, based on verified finances. It tells you your real budget and signals to sellers you're serious. Shop multiple lenders — rates and fees vary, and even a fraction of a percent compounds over 30 years. Pre-approval is different from a quick "pre-qualification"; get the real thing before house-hunting seriously.

## Step 5: House-hunt within budget

With a firm number, search for homes at or below your comfortable maximum — not the top of your approval. Factor in the *full* monthly cost: principal, interest, taxes, insurance, and maintenance (budget ~1–2% of value per year), plus any HOA fees. The [mortgage calculator](/calculators/mortgage-calculator/) shows the real monthly payment including taxes and insurance. Also weigh location and [commute cost](/calculators/commute-cost-calculator/) — a cheaper home far away isn't always cheaper.

## Step 6: Make an offer and get inspections

When you find the one, your agent helps you make an offer. Once accepted:

- **Get a home inspection.** Never skip this — it can reveal costly problems and give you room to renegotiate or walk away.
- **The lender orders an appraisal** to confirm the home is worth the price.
- **Negotiate repairs or credits** based on the inspection.

## Step 7: Close and get the keys

Closing is the final step: you sign the paperwork, pay your down payment and closing costs, and the home becomes yours. Review the closing statement carefully and make sure the numbers match what you were quoted. Then — keys in hand.

## Common first-timer mistakes to avoid

- **Buying at the top of your approval** — leaves no breathing room.
- **Forgetting closing costs and maintenance** — the price is just the start.
- **Skipping the inspection** to win a bidding war — risky and potentially very costly.
- **Not shopping lenders** — the first rate is rarely the best.
- **Draining the emergency fund** — you'll want it for the surprises ownership brings.
- **Making big financial moves before closing** — a new loan or job change can derail your mortgage approval.

## The bottom line

A first home purchase is a marathon: know your true budget, save the down payment *plus* closing costs and a preserved emergency fund, get your credit and DTI loan-ready, secure a real pre-approval from the best lender, buy below your ceiling, never skip the inspection, and check every number at closing. Take it step by step and the process becomes manageable — and you'll walk into your first home on solid financial footing rather than stretched to the limit.
