Negotiating a raise is one of the highest-return uses of an hour in your entire financial life — and most people never do it. A single successful negotiation can be worth far more than years of budgeting, and because future raises and bonuses build on your new base, the effect compounds for decades. Here's how to do it well.

## Why it matters more than you think

A raise isn't a one-time gain. Because future percentage raises, bonuses, and often retirement matches are calculated off your salary, a higher base compounds over your whole career. Negotiating an extra $5,000 early can be worth [six figures](/calculators/pay-raise-calculator/) over a working lifetime once you account for all the future raises stacked on top of it. Skipping the conversation isn't "playing it safe" — it's leaving large, compounding money on the table.

## Build your case before you ask

The worst pitch is "I need more money" or "I've been here a while." The best is a documented case that you deliver *more value* than your pay reflects:

- **Quantify your impact.** Revenue influenced, costs saved, projects shipped, problems solved. Numbers beat adjectives. Keep a running "brag file" through the year so you're not scrambling.
- **Benchmark the market.** Research what your role pays elsewhere (salary sites, recruiters, peers). Knowing the market rate turns your ask from a feeling into a fact.
- **Factor in inflation.** If your pay hasn't kept up with rising prices, that's a legitimate, objective part of the case — check with our [salary inflation calculator](/calculators/salary-inflation-calculator/). Frame it as "a cost-of-living adjustment *plus* recognition of my expanded contribution."

## What number to name

- **Aim slightly high, anchored to data.** Negotiations tend to settle below the first number, so a well-justified higher anchor pulls the result up. But keep it credible — tie it to market rate and your impact.
- **Give a specific figure or tight range,** not "whatever you think is fair." Specific numbers signal you've done your homework.
- **Think in total compensation,** not just base salary. If base is capped, negotiate a bonus, more equity, a better title (which raises your next job's offers), extra leave, or a development budget — the [pay raise calculator](/calculators/pay-raise-calculator/) shows what any version is worth after taxes and over the years it compounds.

## The conversation itself

- **Timing matters.** Ask after a clear win, at review time, or when you've taken on more responsibility — not during a company crisis.
- **Lead with value, then the ask.** "Over the past year I did X, Y, and Z, which delivered [result]. Based on that and the market rate for this role, I'd like to discuss moving my salary to $____."
- **Stay collaborative, not combative.** You're inviting your manager to solve a problem *with* you, framed around your contribution — not issuing a threat.
- **Then be quiet.** After you name the number, stop talking. Silence is uncomfortable, and letting the other side fill it is a classic, effective tactic.

## Handling the response

- **If yes:** get it in writing, and thank them. Done well.
- **If "not now":** ask exactly what would justify a raise and by when. Turn a "no" into a concrete, measurable path with a follow-up date. Get the criteria in writing so it can't quietly evaporate.
- **If a hard no with no path:** that's valuable information about your ceiling here. The strongest long-term salary lever is often changing jobs — external offers routinely beat internal raises, which is worth weighing honestly. When an outside offer does arrive, compare it on *total* value — match, PTO, insurance, commute — with the [job offer comparison calculator](/calculators/job-offer-comparison-calculator/), not just on base salary; the component-by-component gap is also exactly the number that makes a counteroffer conversation concrete.

## Overcome the fear

Most people avoid this out of fear it'll damage the relationship. In reality, a professional, well-prepared ask rarely backfires — managers expect it, and reasonable ones respect it. The worst realistic outcome is usually "not yet," which costs you nothing and often opens a path. Weigh that small risk against the compounding, career-long value of getting paid what you're worth, and the math is overwhelmingly in favor of asking.

## The bottom line

Preparation beats charisma. Document your impact, learn the market rate, factor in inflation, name a specific well-anchored number, and deliver it calmly at the right time. It's a brief, uncomfortable conversation with an outsized, compounding payoff — quite possibly the best-paid hour of your year.
