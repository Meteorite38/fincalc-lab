Losing a job is stressful, but it's a survivable, temporary situation — especially with a clear plan. Panic leads to bad decisions; a methodical response protects both your finances and your peace of mind. Here's what to do, roughly in order.

## First: don't panic, and take stock

In the first days, resist any drastic financial moves. Instead, calmly assess your position:

- **Calculate your runway.** How many months can your savings cover your expenses? Our [savings runway calculator](/calculators/savings-runway-calculator/) gives you the number — and knowing it replaces vague dread with a concrete timeline to work within.
- **List your resources.** Savings, any severance, a partner's income, and benefits you may qualify for.
- **List your obligations.** Rent/mortgage, utilities, minimum debt payments, insurance, food.

Knowing exactly where you stand is the foundation for every decision that follows.

## Switch to a bare-bones survival budget

Immediately cut spending to essentials only. This isn't forever — it's to stretch your runway as far as possible:

- **Keep:** housing, utilities, food, insurance, minimum debt payments, transport for job hunting.
- **Cut or pause:** subscriptions, dining out, discretionary shopping, non-essential memberships, travel.
- **Contact providers proactively.** Many lenders, landlords, and utilities have hardship or deferral programs — ask *before* you miss a payment. It's far easier to arrange help in advance than to recover from a missed payment.

Every dollar you don't spend extends your runway and reduces pressure.

## Claim every benefit and buffer you can

- **File for unemployment benefits** (or your local equivalent) immediately — there's often a waiting period, so don't delay. It's support you've likely paid into.
- **Understand your severance and any final pay,** including unused leave.
- **Check health insurance continuity** — losing job-based coverage often triggers options you must act on within a deadline.
- **Tap the emergency fund — that's what it's for.** Don't feel guilty using the [emergency fund](/calculators/emergency-fund-calculator/) you built; this is precisely the emergency it exists for.

## Protect your long-term finances

While handling the short term, avoid moves that cause lasting damage:

- **Don't cash out retirement accounts** except as a true last resort — the [early withdrawal calculator](/calculators/401k-early-withdrawal-calculator/) shows the 30-45% immediate haircut plus the decades of lost [compounding](/articles/how-compound-interest-builds-wealth/). When the paperwork about your old 401(k) arrives, choose a direct rollover, never the check — the [rollover options guide](/articles/401k-rollover-options-job-change/) walks the decision.
- **Don't panic-sell investments** to raise cash if you can avoid it, especially in a downturn (job losses often coincide with market drops — the [sequence risk](/articles/sequence-of-returns-risk/) that's exactly why you hold a cash buffer).
- **Prioritize minimum debt payments** to protect your credit, but pause any *extra* debt payments to preserve cash.
- **Be cautious with new debt.** Credit can bridge a short gap, but high-interest debt compounds a hard situation. Use it sparingly and strategically.

## Focus energy on the income side

The fastest way out is new income:

- **Treat the job search as your job** — consistent daily effort, using your network (which finds most jobs), not just applications.
- **Consider interim income** — temporary, part-time, freelance, or gig work can slow the drain on savings and extend your runway while you search for the right role.
- **Invest in yourself** if you have time — a skill that raises your marketability can turn a setback into a step up.

## The mindset

A job loss feels like a crisis but is usually a temporary chapter. Households that come through well aren't the luckiest — they're the ones who stayed calm, cut fast, claimed their benefits, protected their long-term assets, and put their energy into the next income. Your runway buys you time; use it deliberately rather than anxiously, and treat the setback as a bridge to what's next.

## The bottom line

If you lose your job: breathe, calculate your runway, cut to a survival budget, claim every benefit, use (don't feel guilty about) your emergency fund, protect your retirement and investments from panic moves, and pour your energy into new income. A clear plan turns a frightening event into a manageable transition — and the safety net you built in good times is what carries you through.
